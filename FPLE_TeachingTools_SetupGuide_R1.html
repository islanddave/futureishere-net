🎓 Step 1 — LearnCube
🎓
LearnCube — Your Classroom
5 steps · your primary teaching platform
+
TL;DRSign up for Virtual Classroom Pro + Premium ESL (~$27/mo). Set your profile. Copy your permanent classroom link — you'll paste it into Acuity in the next section. That link is the critical thing to save.
1
Sign up at learncube.com Go to learncube.com → click Get Started → on the pricing page, choose Virtual Classroom Pro → when asked about content, add the Premium ESL add-on. This is the plan that includes CEFR A1–C2 content. ~$27/month total.
2
Complete your instructor profile After sign-up, click your name or avatar (top-right corner) → Profile Settings → fill in: display name, upload a photo, write a short bio (2–3 sentences), and set your timezone. Students see this — warm and professional.
3
Enable student access and copy your classroom link In your dashboard, click Settings (gear icon, top-right or left sidebar) → click Student Portal → toggle Enable Student Portal to ON → copy the URL shown under "Your Portal Link." ⭐ Save this link in your notes app or a sticky note right now. It's permanent, never changes, and you'll paste it into Acuity in Step 2.
4
Explore the Content Library In the left sidebar, click Content Library → use the CEFR Level filter → browse and bookmark 3–5 exercises each for A1, A2, B1, and B2 to start. (The Content Library is inside your dashboard after login — not a public URL.)
5
Run a test session with yourself Click Classroom → Start Session → test your microphone and camera in the permissions prompt → add one homework item from the Content Library → then open your Student Portal link in a second browser tab to verify what students see. Takes about 10 minutes. Catches tech issues before your students encounter them.
✅
Done when You've run a test session, found content by CEFR level, and your classroom link is saved somewhere you can find it in 5 seconds.
📅 Step 2 — Acuity Scheduling
📅
Acuity — Booking & Payment
7 steps · students book, pay, and get everything they need automatically
+
TL;DRSign up ($20/mo), connect Stripe and Google Calendar, create your three session types, then paste your LearnCube link into every confirmation email. That paste is the only manual connection between these two tools — do it once, it's permanent.
1
Sign up at acuityscheduling.com Go to acuityscheduling.com → click Get Started Free → create your account → when prompted to choose a plan, select Emerging ($20/month). This is the plan with Stripe payment, intake forms, and automated reminder emails.
2
Connect Stripe for payment In Acuity's left sidebar, click Business Settings → click Payment Processing → click Connect with Stripe → if you don't have a Stripe account, click Create one (or go directly to dashboard.stripe.com/register) and follow the prompts (takes ~5 minutes) → once connected, Stripe will confirm and return you to Acuity. Students pay automatically at booking. You'll never need to send an invoice or chase a payment.
3
Connect Google Calendar In the left sidebar, click Business Settings → click Integrations → scroll to find Google Calendar → click Add New → click Sign in with Google → choose your Google account → click Allow on the permissions screen → once connected, select which Google Calendar to sync to (your main teaching calendar) from the dropdown → click Save. From now on, every booking automatically blocks your Google Calendar. No manual updates needed.
4
Create your three session types In the left sidebar, click Appointment Types → click + New Appointment Type → fill in the details below. Repeat for all three:
A
Trial Session — 30 min, set a lower/introductory price, description: "30-minute intro session for new students."
B
Individual Session — 60 min, set your full hourly rate.
C
Group Session — 60 min, set your group rate (typically lower per student than individual).
5
Paste your LearnCube link into each confirmation email In Appointment Types, click a session type → click Edit → scroll to the Email Templates tab → find Confirmation Email → in the email body, add a line like: "Join your classroom here: [paste your LearnCube link]" → click Save. Repeat for all three session types. ⚠️ This is the only manual connection between Acuity and LearnCube. Once done, it's permanent — students automatically receive the link every time they book.
6
Add an intake form to Trial and Individual session types In Appointment Types, click a session type → click Edit → click the Intake Forms tab → click + Add a new intake form → add these fields:
1
What is your native language? (text field)
2
How would you describe your current English level? (dropdown: Beginner / Elementary / Intermediate / Upper-Intermediate / Advanced)
3
What are your main goals for learning English? (text area)
4
Which days/times work best for you? (text field)
This intake data feeds directly into your Claude student profiles. See the Connections section for how to get it there.
7
Set up automated reminder emails In the left sidebar, click Business Settings → click Email & Reminder Settings → find Appointment Reminders → enable it → set First reminder: 24 hours before → click + Add another reminder → set Second reminder: 1 hour before → in each reminder email template, add your LearnCube link with the note: "Your classroom link: [link]" → click Save. Students who get reminders show up. Students who don't, sometimes don't.
✅
Done when A student can find your page, choose a session type, pay, and automatically receive a confirmation with the LearnCube link and a reminder — without you doing anything.

Phase 1 — Get your printed materials into Claude

1
Scan or photograph your printed lesson plans For each lesson type you have a printed plan for: use your phone camera or a scanner app (e.g. Adobe Scan or iPhone's built-in document scan in Notes → tap the camera icon → Scan Document) → save as a PDF or clear photo. One file per lesson type is ideal. If you have a stack, start with one representative example of each type.
2
For existing PowerPoints — export as PDF Open the PowerPoint → File → Export → Create PDF/XPS (Windows) or File → Save As → PDF (Mac) → save. Claude reads PDF slides fully — every slide becomes readable text it can reason about. Keep the original .pptx too; you'll upload that separately for active lesson use.
3
Upload to Claude and extract the template structure Open a new conversation inside your FPLE Teaching Workspace project → click the paperclip or upload icon → attach the scanned lesson plan (or exported PDF) → then send this prompt:
📋 Prompt to use "This is one of my existing lesson plans. Please study its structure carefully and extract a reusable blank template from it — keeping all section headings, time allocations, and field names exactly as I use them. Label it as a [Speaking / Writing / Reading / Listening / Grammar] lesson plan template. Show me the result and ask me if I want to adjust anything before we finalize it."
Claude will read your layout, mirror your own terminology and structure, and hand you back a clean template in your voice — not a generic one. If the scan has 4 sections, the template has 4 sections. If you call it "Production Task" instead of "Speaking Practice," the template uses that name.
4
Use an existing PPT to generate a lesson plan — and reverse-engineer the template If a PowerPoint already represents a complete lesson, Claude can work from the slides rather than a written plan. Upload the exported PDF of that PPT and send:
📋 Prompt to use "These slides are a complete [B1 Speaking / B2 Writing / etc.] lesson I've already taught. Please reconstruct the lesson plan from these slides — what was the goal, what was the sequence, what did each slide ask students to do? Then use that reconstruction to create a blank template I can reuse for future [lesson type] lessons at this level."
This works particularly well when your printed plan and the PPT were built together — Claude can cross-reference both uploads in the same conversation and produce a template that reflects how you actually run that lesson type.
5
Save each finalized template as its own file and upload to the project Once Claude produces a template you're happy with, copy it into a new Google Doc or Word document. Use this naming convention so Claude always knows which template to use:

🔗 The Two Manual Connections

Connecting the Tools
Only two manual steps exist in the whole stack
+
TL;DREverything in this stack connects automatically — except these two things. Connection 1 you've already done (if you followed Acuity Step 5). Connection 2 is a 30-second forward or one optional Zapier rule.
⚠️ Manual Connection 1
Acuity → LearnCube
No native integration exists between these two tools. Students who book won't automatically receive your LearnCube classroom link unless you add it to the confirmation email.
✅ Fix (one-time, permanent): In each Acuity session type → Edit → Email Templates → Confirmation Email → paste your LearnCube link. You did this in Acuity Step 5. Once saved, every future booking includes the link automatically. Nothing more to do.
⚠️ Manual Connection 2
Acuity Intake Form → Claude Student Profiles
When a new student books and fills in your intake form, that information arrives as an Acuity email. It doesn't automatically reach your Claude student profiles doc — you need to move it there.
Option A — Manual (best for under ~10 students):
When you receive a new booking email from Acuity: open a new chat in your FPLE Teaching Workspace project → paste or forward the intake email contents → say: "Add this new student to the profiles doc." Claude creates their profile section. Takes 60 seconds.

Option B — Automated via Zapier (optional, for 10+ students):
In Zapier (free tier works), create one rule: Trigger: New Appointment in Acuity → Action: Append row to a Google Sheet named "FPLE Student Intake Log." Claude reads your Drive, so it can then access this log when you ask. Set-up time: ~20 minutes, one-time.
Everything else is automatic after initial setup — Stripe payment processing, Google Calendar blocking, reminder emails, Drive/Gmail access in Claude, LearnCube session notes. These two are genuinely the only manual steps in the whole stack.

Stay Organized — The System That Works for You
Read this first · the more organized your setup, the smarter Claude gets · automatically
+
TL;DROrganization isn't about being tidy — it's about making the system do the remembering so you don't have to. The rules below are short and specific. Follow the naming conventions and folder structure once, and Claude can find anything. Don't follow them, and it can't.
⚠️ This section is the highest-leverage thing you can do after setup. Every prompt you give Claude works better when files behind it are named consistently and live in the right folder. A messy Drive = a confused Claude = you doing more work, not less. Five minutes of organization now saves hours of frustration later.

🤖 Claude — How Your Project Is Structured (3 Layers)

Think of your Claude project as three distinct layers. Keep them clean and separate. Mixing them up is the most common reason Claude gives weird or unhelpful answers.

📎 Layer 1 — Project Knowledge Base
The files uploaded to FPLE Teaching Workspace via + Add content. This is Claude's permanent reference memory — lesson plan templates, student profiles, business overview. These are reference documents only. Do not upload individual lesson plans or one-off notes here. Treat this like a filing cabinet, not a notepad.
💬 Layer 2 — Named Chats
Each conversation inside the project. One chat = one student = one session purpose. Claude auto-names chats with useless titles like "Can you help me with..." — always rename before closing. Format: Olena — Speaking — Job Interviews — Apr 2025. In 3 months you'll thank yourself.
⚙️ Layer 3 — Project Instruction
The standing instruction in project Settings → Project Instructions. This is the "always remember this" layer — your teaching persona, student base, template preference. Set it once during setup. Only edit it when something fundamentally changes about your business.
🔄 What lives where — quick rule
Project files = permanent templates & profiles only.
Named chats = individual session work.
Google Drive = completed lesson plans, session notes, PPTs.
Never paste a full completed lesson plan into the project knowledge base — it bloats Claude's context and degrades response quality over time.

Claude org rules — the never/always list

🚫
Never create a separate project per studentOne project only. Claude needs to see all students together to cross-reference, avoid repeating content, and spot patterns across your teaching. Splitting into multiple projects breaks all of that.
🚫
Never leave a chat with Claude's auto-generated name"Can you help me make a lesson plan" tells you nothing in two weeks. At the end of every lesson plan chat, paste this single line before you close the tab:

"Suggest a title for this chat using format: [Student] — [Level] — [Type] — [Topic] — [Month Year]"
Claude returns something like Olena — B1 — Speaking — Job Interviews — Apr 2025. Copy it, click the chat title at the top, paste, done. Five seconds. The Project Instruction below also makes Claude offer this automatically every time — so you don't even have to remember to ask.
🚫
Never mix two students in the same chatStart a fresh chat for each student each session. If you start asking about Olena halfway through a Darya chat, Claude gets confused and so will you when you look back.
🚫
Never upload a file with a vague name"Scan1.pdf", "lesson (2).docx", "new template" — Claude can't reliably reference these. Name every file before uploading: FPLE_LP_Grammar_V1.docx. If the name doesn't tell you exactly what it is, rename it first.
🚫
Never accumulate stale files in the project knowledge baseIf you upload a draft template and later make a better version, delete the old one from the project. Claude reads all uploaded files — outdated versions create contradictions and confusion.
✅
Always update the Student Profiles doc, not a chatPost-session notes belong in the Profiles doc — not buried in a conversation. Use the post-session update prompt, confirm Claude updated the doc, close the chat. The doc is permanent; the chat is temporary.
✅
Always do the 5-minute weekly reset — automate the reminderEvery Friday or Sunday. It catches everything that drifted — un-updated profiles, un-renamed chats, lesson plans not yet saved to Drive. Five minutes a week prevents a two-hour backlog every month.

Automate the reminder with Google Calendar: Go to calendar.google.com → create a recurring event every Sunday at a time you'll actually have 5 minutes → in the event description, paste the full weekly reset prompt (from the prompt box below). The reminder fires, the prompt is right there, open Claude, paste, done. This is the most reliable "automation" in your current stack — zero additional tools, zero cost, always fires.

🎓 LearnCube — Keep the Classroom Clean

👤 Student name consistency

🔖 Content Library — less is more

30-second sanity check on every lesson plan you generate
+
TL;DRAsk Claude to review its own lesson plan before you use it. Catches level mismatches, unclear instructions, and missing transitions — before students see them.

(2) Are the timing allocations realistic for a B1 student working in a second language? (3) Does the lesson flow logically from warm-up to close? A 30-second review loop catches this before the lesson, not during it.

ESL Lesson Risks — and How to Catch Them Early
Common construction problems and the Claude prompts that prevent them
+
TL;DRWell-planned ESL lessons can still have hidden problems — level mismatch, grammar overload, Ukrainian L1 interference, or activities that only work in a classroom. These prompts flag them before they cost you a session.
⚠️
Risk 1 — Level creep A B1 lesson quietly contains B2 vocabulary or grammar, frustrating the student without either of you knowing why.
Fix prompt Claude: "Scan this lesson plan for any vocabulary items or grammar structures above B1 CEFR level. List each one and suggest a simpler alternative at B1."
⚠️
Risk 2 — Grammar overload Introducing two or three grammar points in one lesson — common when adapting printed materials — prevents any of them from sticking.
Fix prompt Claude: "This lesson plan touches on more than one grammar point. Which is the most important for this student's current level and goals? Help me restructure the lesson to focus only on that one, and suggest when to schedule the others."
⚠️
Risk 3 — Ukrainian L1 interference Ukrainian speakers have predictable difficulties: articles (a/the — absent in Ukrainian), aspect in past tense, prepositions, and some false cognates. These may not be obvious in a plan built without that lens.
Fix prompt Claude: "Review this lesson plan for grammar points or vocabulary that are typically difficult for Ukrainian L1 speakers. Flag each potential difficulty and suggest a brief pre-emptive explanation or comparison I can add to the lesson."
⚠️
Risk 4 — Online delivery gap Activities designed for physical classrooms — pass this to your partner, write on the board, listen to the classroom recording — break in an online session without adaptation.
Fix prompt Claude: "This plan was adapted from a printed lesson. Identify any activities that assume a physical classroom and suggest a practical online equivalent for each one using LearnCube's virtual classroom features."
⚠️
Risk 5 — Homework drop-off Homework that is too open-ended, too long, or not clearly connected to the lesson goal gets ignored — especially by adult learners with full-time jobs and families.
Fix prompt Claude: "Review the homework task in this lesson plan. Is it specific enough that the student knows exactly what to do, short enough to complete in under 20 minutes, and clearly connected to today's lesson goal? If not, suggest a revised homework task."
